Adopt a real seal and follow its GPS-tracked journey. From the ice floes of Bohai Bay to the kelp forests of Hokkaido, the Bering Sea, and the Baltic — every seal tells a different story about our ocean.

Banban

Bānbān
3 years old Male Active Explorer

"A lively and curious young seal, born on the ice floes of Liaodong Bay. Always eager to discover new foraging grounds."

Banban was born during a cold February and still remembers the first time he slipped into the freezing water. He's growing fast and loves to chase small fish near the kelp beds.

Diandian

Diǎndiǎn
7 years old Female Experienced Mother

"A seasoned seal mother who returns to Bohai Bay's breeding grounds every year without fail. She knows every safe migration route by heart."

Diandian has raised three pups over her lifetime. She carries the knowledge of seven migration seasons and always guides younger seals through the Bohai Strait safely.

Dayuan

Dàyuán
12 years old Male Elder Bull

"A massive and calm elder bull. His movement patterns record over a decade of environmental changes in the Bohai Bay ecosystem."

Dayuan is one of the largest seals in his cohort. He has survived fishing gear entanglements and severe ice reduction events. His satellite data is invaluable to researchers.

Shiretoko

Spotted Seal · Hokkaido, Japan
5 years oldFemaleDrift-Ice Diver

"A powerful diver who follows the Sea of Okhotsk drift ice south each winter, then hunts the kelp forests of Shiretoko's UNESCO coastline in summer."

Shiretoko was satellite-tagged near Monbetsu, Hokkaido in February — part of a joint Japan-Russia telemetry study on Phoca largha. She consistently dives below 200 m chasing walleye pollock beneath the ice edge.

Nunivak

Spotted Seal · Bering Sea, Alaska
8 years oldMaleIce-Edge Hunter

"A battle-scarred adult male whose GPS tracks mirror the retreating sea-ice edge across the Bering shelf — a living indicator of Arctic change."

Nunivak carries a SPOT satellite tag deployed by NOAA's Alaska Fisheries Science Center. His annual migration spans over 2,000 km from the Bering ice pack to summer estuaries near the Yukon-Kuskokwim Delta.

Saimaa

Saimaa Ringed Seal · Lake Saimaa, Finland
4 years oldFemaleFreshwater Relic

"One of the world's rarest seals — ~440 individuals — isolated in a Finnish freshwater lake since the last ice age. Her entire world is 4,400 km² of archipelago."

Saimaa is tracked under the WWF Finland / Metsahallitus telemetry programme. Her snowdrift birth lair was confirmed by camera trap in January. Every individual matters when a population is this small.

Okrug

Baltic Grey Seal · Archipelago Sea, Finland
11 years oldMaleLong-Distance Forager

"A large Baltic grey seal whose published GPS tracks show extraordinary 800 km foraging excursions from his haul-out rocks near Uto island."

Okrug was tagged at Uto as part of the University of Helsinki / ICES Baltic seal monitoring programme. His deep foraging dives off the Polish coast reach 130 m — unusually deep for the shallow Baltic Sea.

Spotted Seal Encyclopedia

Classification

Scientific name: Phoca largha. Family: Phocidae (true seals). Order: Carnivora. Class: Mammalia.

IUCN Status

Vulnerable (VU) — Population declining due to habitat loss and climate change.

Distribution

Found across the western North Pacific, primarily in the Bohai Sea, Yellow Sea, Sea of Japan, and the Sea of Okhotsk. China's Bohai Sea hosts the only breeding population on pack ice south of the Arctic Circle.

Physical Characteristics

  • Length: 150–170 cm; Weight: 85–130 kg
  • Coat: Light grey with dark spots — their namesake pattern
  • Pups are born with white "lanugo" coats for 3–4 weeks
  • Lifespan: up to 35 years in the wild

Breeding

Spotted seals breed on pack ice from late January to March in Liaodong Bay. Each female gives birth to a single pup. Nursing lasts 3–4 weeks, during which the pup grows rapidly on fat-rich milk.

Foraging

They feed on small schooling fish (walleye pollock, capelin, sand lance), crustaceans, and cephalopods. Adults can fast for weeks during breeding season, relying on stored blubber.

Molting

Annual molt occurs in spring (April–June) when seals haul out on beaches or rocks to shed their old coat. They are sluggish during this period.

Diving Ability

Capable of dives exceeding 300 meters and holding breath for up to 20 minutes. Their blood has a high oxygen-carrying capacity compared to terrestrial mammals.

The Bohai-Yellow Sea spotted seal population undergoes a clear seasonal migration pattern:

Winter–Spring (Jan–Apr): Breeding in Liaodong Bay

Seals gather on pack ice in northern Liaodong Bay (40–41 N) to give birth and nurse pups. This is the only known pack-ice breeding habitat for this population.

Spring (Apr–May): Northward Dispersal

After weaning, seals move through the Bohai Strait (approx. 38–39 N), passing between the Liaodong Peninsula and Shandong Peninsula.

Summer–Autumn (Jun–Dec): Yellow Sea Foraging

Seals disperse into the Yellow Sea (37–38 N), exploiting rich fish stocks associated with the Yellow Sea Cold Water Mass. Some individuals travel to Korean and Japanese waters.

Return Migration

As temperatures drop in November–December, seals begin returning north to the Bohai Sea to repeat the cycle.

Climate Change

Rising sea temperatures are reducing sea ice extent and duration in Liaodong Bay — the only pack-ice breeding area for this population. Poor ice years lead to dramatically reduced pup survival rates.

Habitat Destruction

Coastal development, land reclamation, and pollution have degraded nearshore habitats critical for hauling out and molting.

Fisheries Interactions

Bycatch in gillnets and trawls poses a significant mortality risk. Prey depletion from overfishing reduces food availability.

Human Disturbance

Boat traffic, tourism, and aquaculture noise can disturb resting and nursing seals, causing pup abandonment. China's population estimates range from 1,000 to 8,000 individuals — significantly reduced from historical counts.

  • Maintain distance: If you spot a seal hauled out, stay at least 100 meters away. Do not approach, feed, or touch them.
  • Report sightings: Report seal sightings and entangled/injured animals to local wildlife authorities or rescue organizations.
  • Sustainable seafood: Choose sustainably sourced fish certified by MSC or similar standards to reduce pressure on seal prey species.
  • Reduce plastic: Marine debris can injure seals. Reduce single-use plastics and join beach clean-ups.
  • Support policy: Advocate for stronger marine protected areas around key seal habitats in the Bohai and Yellow Sea.
  • Spread the word: Share what you've learned. Public awareness is a critical tool for conservation.
  • Support research: Donate to organizations conducting seal telemetry research — data like this app's tracks are how we understand their needs.
